Learn About Pollutants

PM 2.5 — Fine Particulate Matter

PM 2.5 refers to tiny particles or droplets in the air that are 2.5 micrometres or smaller in diameter — roughly 30 times smaller than a human hair.

Health Effects

Can penetrate deep into the lungs and enter the bloodstream. Linked to heart disease, stroke, lung cancer, and respiratory illness.

Sources

Vehicle exhaust, power plants, wildfires, industrial processes, and burning wood or coal.

WHO Safe Limit

5 µg/m³ annual mean

PM 10 — Coarse Particulate Matter

PM 10 refers to inhalable particles with diameters of 10 micrometres or smaller. They are larger than PM 2.5 but still pose health risks.

Health Effects

Can irritate the eyes, nose, and throat. Long-term exposure is associated with respiratory and cardiovascular disease.

Sources

Dust from roads and construction sites, pollen, mould spores, and crushing or grinding operations.

WHO Safe Limit

15 µg/m³ annual mean

NO₂ — Nitrogen Dioxide

Nitrogen dioxide is a reddish-brown gas with a sharp smell. It forms when fuel is burned at high temperatures.

Health Effects

Irritates the respiratory system. High levels can worsen asthma and increase susceptibility to respiratory infections.

Sources

Road vehicles, power stations, industrial plants, and domestic heating appliances.

WHO Safe Limit

10 µg/m³ annual mean

O₃ — Ground-Level Ozone

Ground-level ozone is not emitted directly but forms when NOx and VOCs react in sunlight. It is different from the protective ozone layer high in the atmosphere.

Health Effects

Can trigger chest pain, coughing, and throat irritation. It aggravates bronchitis, emphysema, and asthma, and can reduce lung function.

Sources

Formed from reactions between NOx and VOCs emitted by vehicles, industry, and solvents in the presence of sunlight.

WHO Safe Limit

60 µg/m³ peak season daily max (8-hour mean)

CO — Carbon Monoxide

Carbon monoxide is a colourless, odourless gas that is toxic at high concentrations. It is produced by incomplete combustion of fossil fuels.

Health Effects

Reduces the blood's ability to carry oxygen. Symptoms include headaches, dizziness, and nausea. High concentrations can be fatal.

Sources

Vehicle exhaust, faulty boilers and gas appliances, burning wood or charcoal, and industrial furnaces.

WHO Safe Limit

4 mg/m³ 24-hour mean
